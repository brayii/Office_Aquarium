# Office Aquarium
## Player Manual - Save Version 40

Office Aquarium is an autonomous technology-company simulation. You are the CEO of a company building hardware and software. Employees control themselves; your job is to watch the organization, understand what is happening, respond to important communications, and keep the company healthy enough to survive and grow.

This manual describes the current save version 40 build. The game remains a standalone offline application and does not require an API key or online service.

The current build uses a main `Office_Aquarium.html` file plus ordered JavaScript files under `src/`. Keep the folder structure together, including `assets/audio` for optional sound, when moving the game to another PC or mobile device.

---

# Current Build Notes

The current build uses stricter staffing and crisis rules.

If one active project is short on staff, that usually means the company has a project or workforce problem to watch. It should not automatically mean the whole company is in crisis. A true company crisis requires broader sustained evidence, such as several affected projects, failed recruiting, critical roles left open, worsening delivery, major financial stress, or repeated unresolved shortages.

Project cards report the organization's current view of staffing, blockers, backlog, timing, health, risk, commercial status, and revenue potential. Some hidden details are imperfectly known by employees and managers, so normal play presents executive reporting rather than raw internal values.

# 1. The Basic Idea

You do not control individual employees.

Employees decide when to work, test, collaborate, meet, socialize, complain, take breaks, or go home. Their decisions are affected by their personalities, skills, goals, energy, stress, morale, relationships, beliefs, memories, work assignments, and what they have learned from previous outcomes.

Your job is to:

- watch the office
- read CEO communications
- choose strategic responses
- monitor cash, trust, customers, product progress, manufacturing, and Investor Confidence
- protect employee health and company culture
- decide CEO-level issues such as launch timing, project direction, hiring policy, headcount approval, restructuring, layoffs, crisis response, and strategic trade-offs
- survive long enough to build a successful company

The game is designed around indirect leadership. You influence the company; you do not puppeteer it.

The current build includes project portfolios, derived operating health, hiring policy and recruiting pipelines, investor relations, valuation trends, customer and market intelligence, executive messages, company history, story threads, evidence-based Institutional Learning, professional social memory, visible conversations, and People & Culture reporting.

---

# 2. What Influence Means

In this manual, "influence" does not mean clicking an employee and commanding them to behave differently.

The CEO influences the conditions around the company:

- which strategic trade-offs are approved
- what hiring policy allows
- which headcount requests receive approval
- whether a project is funded, paused, narrowed, validated, cancelled, or launched
- whether leadership protects quality, speed, runway, people, customers, or innovation
- whether the organization sees decisions as fair, transparent, reckless, supportive, or short-sighted

The AI handles the behavior behind the scenes. Employees, managers, HR, departments, and the board react through the simulation. They decide when to collaborate, recover, report, suppress, hire, coach, mentor, resign, escalate, or keep working.

You set direction. The organization lives with it.

---

# 3. How to Play Office Aquarium

Office Aquarium is not a game about controlling employees.

Think of yourself as a real CEO.

Your employees already know how to do their jobs. They will solve many problems without you. Your responsibility is deciding which problems deserve executive attention, which trade-offs are worth making, and what kind of company your decisions create over time.

A successful CEO does not react to every single signal. A successful CEO learns to recognize patterns.

Ask yourself:

- Is this an employee problem, a team problem, or a CEO-level problem?
- Is the company solving this locally, or is the issue growing?
- Am I trading short-term speed for long-term damage?
- Are employees quiet because things are healthy, or because they are afraid to report?
- Is this decision about cash, trust, quality, culture, or survival?

Every decision has a cost. Sometimes the best leadership move is decisive action. Sometimes it is patience. Sometimes it is protecting people from a plan that looks efficient but would quietly break the company.

# 4. The Office Is Alive

The company continues thinking and working even when the CEO Inbox is quiet.

Employees communicate with each other. Teams handle many issues locally. Reports can be delayed, softened, escalated, or suppressed. Rumors spread when information is incomplete. Opportunities can be discovered by employees before they become formal strategy. The newspaper reports what actually happened inside the simulation.

This means silence is not emptiness.

A quiet day may mean:

- employees are making progress without needing you
- a team resolved a blocker locally
- reports have not reached CEO level yet
- people are still forming beliefs about a risk
- an opportunity is developing inside current work
- a problem exists but has not become strategic

Your job is to read the whole company, not just the inbox.

Use department briefings, internal reports, employee profiles, story threads, and the weekly paper together. The company is most understandable when you look for chains of cause and effect rather than isolated events.

---

# 5. Things You Cannot Control

You cannot directly control employees.

You cannot:

- tell a specific employee to write code
- tell someone exactly when to take a break
- manually assign every task
- stop employees from talking
- force collaboration
- directly increase productivity
- guarantee that a report reaches you
- remove uncertainty from the company
- personally promote, coach, reassign, or fire someone outside a formal CEO-level decision
- choose ordinary candidates after HR opens an approved role
- directly set morale, stress, skill, productivity, loyalty, or learning

Instead, you influence the conditions the AI reacts to through:

- formal CEO memo decisions
- hiring policy and headcount approval
- project funding, pause, validation, scope, cancellation, or launch choices
- quality, speed, revenue, people, runway, and customer trade-offs
- restructuring and layoff decisions when they reach CEO level
- crisis responses
- consistency, fairness, transparency, and trust over time

Managers and HR handle ordinary coaching, onboarding, performance management, recruiting, and internal reassignment unless an issue becomes strategic enough for the CEO Inbox. The player does not micromanage those actions.

This is the core challenge of Office Aquarium. You are not operating a machine. You are leading an organization.

# 7. Your First 15 Minutes

If this is your first run, do not try to optimize immediately. Spend the first few minutes learning how the company behaves.

1. Start a new company.
2. Let the simulation run at 1x for a few minutes.
3. Watch employees move through the office and read their thoughts.
4. Open the Employees panel and inspect two or three people.
5. Read the Department Briefings on the Company screen.
6. Look at Operating Health, Project Portfolio, and Internal Reports.
7. Wait for your first CEO memo instead of trying to force one.
8. Make your first executive decision.
9. Let time pass and watch how employees react.
10. Read the next Office Aquarium Weekly issue.
11. Look for how your decision affected later events, reports, stress, morale, trust, or story threads.

The goal of the first 15 minutes is not to win. The goal is to learn the rhythm of indirect leadership.

# 8. Reading the Company

Every screen is a different lens on the same living organization.

| Screen | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| Office | What people are doing right now |
| Employees | What individuals are thinking and feeling |
| CEO Inbox | Strategic decisions requiring leadership |
| Department Briefings | Operational health by team |
| Internal Reports | What employees have discovered or reported |
| People & Culture | Supported culture, groups, networks, team chemistry, and leadership patterns |
| Story Threads | Cause and effect over time |
| Weekly Newspaper | A historical summary of company events |

Do not rely on only one panel.

A stressed employee may explain a weak department briefing. A quiet inbox may make more sense after reading internal reports. A newspaper story may reveal that a decision from last week is still shaping today's company. A story thread may show that a supplier issue, a blocked work item, and a CEO memo are all part of the same chain.

Office Aquarium is easiest to understand when you combine views.

# 10. Your Main Screens

On desktop, several panels are visible at once.

On mobile, use the tab bar:

- Office
- Employees
- CEO Inbox
- Company
- Paper

## Office

The Office screen shows employees moving through:

- Software Studio
- Hardware Lab
- Meeting Room
- Break Area
- Executive Suite

Employee labels show what they are currently doing. Busy employees display activity animation and thought text appears in the office. Select an employee to open the detailed profile.

Short speech bubbles appear when a real event creates a suitable conversation opportunity. Employees approach one another, greet, discuss the grounded topic, reply, sometimes follow up, say goodbye, and return to what they were doing. The wording varies with personality and may refer to a real shared memory, so coworkers can sound different and later conversations can acknowledge earlier events.

Conversations may cover current work, help, blockers, meetings, deadlines, mentoring, recognition, conflict, repair, news, or celebration. Select a bubble for a larger reading view. Longer lines remain visible longer, and critical work is protected from irrelevant interruptions. The CEO watches these interactions but does not direct them. Private conversations are difficult to overhear; confidential conversations are never displayed.

Roles have a primary workplace, although meetings, collaboration, breaks, and project needs can move employees elsewhere:

| Primary Workplace | Roles |
|---|---|
| Software Studio | Software Engineer, Firmware Engineer, Software QA Engineer, Technical Lead, Software Architect |
| Hardware Lab | Hardware Engineer, Chip Architect, Electrical Engineer, Industrial Designer, Manufacturing Engineer |
| Meeting Room | Product Manager, Manager |
| Executive Suite | Finance Analyst, Director, Vice President |

The founding company starts with eight employees. Successful companies develop through larger staffing plans and can eventually employ every canonical role. Leadership positions appear as the company gains more projects, customers, revenue, valuation, and coordination needs. The CEO approves positions; HR recruits and onboards the people.

## Employees

The Employee Minds panel shows each employee's current condition.

Select an employee to open a detailed profile containing:

- role and traits
- current action
- current thought
- energy
- focus
- morale
- stress
- current work
- beliefs and confidence
- recent messages
- closest coworker
- memories
- milestones
- action reasoning
- opinion of the CEO
- professional relationship summary
- strongest relevant social memories
- culture and group context
- formal or informal leadership influence

## CEO Inbox

The CEO Inbox is your primary decision screen.

It contains:

- Inbox: current communications requiring attention
- Archive: completed communications and recorded decisions

A memo may include:

- subject
- priority
- sender
- a human-readable email or memo body
- summary
- why the issue reached you
- chain of command
- evidence
- what the sender believes
- recommendation
- other viewpoints
- what happens if nothing changes
- what remains uncertain
- decision choices
- potential benefits
- risks
- uncertainty
- estimated confidence

The Inbox does not reveal perfect truth. It shows what the organization believes. A memo may be cautious, optimistic, incomplete, biased by department priorities, or wrong. Compare the memo with Operating Health, Project Portfolio, Department Briefings, Company Story, the newspaper, and previous outcomes.

Current memos are written to feel like executive communication, not diagnostic reports. The main message explains the situation first. Supporting context, evidence, uncertainty, and department viewpoints come after the message. Internal severity scores, evidence IDs, audits, and hidden calculations are not shown in normal play.

Choice titles describe the concrete action you are approving, such as delaying a release, approving headcount, reducing scope, increasing validation, shifting budget, or staying on the current plan. The strategy and trade-offs are explained underneath the title.

Recommendations are not guaranteed to be correct. Finance, Product, Engineering, People, Quality, and the Board may value the same evidence differently. Managers may also merge reports, delay weak signals, handle routine issues locally, or suppress a report. Severe, legal, ethics, safety, whistleblower, retaliation, repeated failed escalation, or protected-channel issues can still bypass normal management.

Normal memos do not show internal AI scores. The standard Inbox is written as executive communication.

Select a decision, then choose Record CEO Decision.

## Company

The Company screen shows operating health, department briefings, project portfolio, workforce pressure, organizational dynamics, People & Culture, company story, story threads, company lessons, and company history when those sections have meaningful content.

Operating Health is a derived executive summary. It is not a separate set of progress bars. Hardware, software, manufacturing, customer, shareholder, morale, trust, cohesion, finance, and portfolio health are calculated from the current projects, employees, departments, customers, manufacturing, finance, board, and market state.

Some values may show N/A when the company does not yet have enough real evidence. For example, Customer Validation may appear before real customers exist, and Manufacturing Readiness means something different from post-launch Manufacturing Health.

There is no separate Company Intelligence or Release Readiness panel in normal play. The simulation builds internal intelligence behind the scenes. Operating Health shows the state, Project Portfolio shows the work, CEO Inbox presents decisions, and the story/newspaper/history views explain what happened.

It also includes Organizational Dynamics, which summarizes long-running leadership and people signals such as:

- next quarterly review
- elevated retention cases
- crisis trajectory
- leadership profile
- organizational momentum

The same area includes Workforce and Financial Pressure. This is the CEO-level view of payroll, operating cost, net cash flow, runway, staffing pressure, retention risk, employees searching, retirement watch, board strikes, PIP status, and company risk. Individual salary details remain hidden in normal play.

People & Culture appears in the Reports workspace and contains four views:

- Culture shows supported norms, direction of change, confidence, and warnings.
- Groups shows informal groups that formed from repeated meaningful interaction.
- Network shows mentoring, conflict, and bridge relationships.
- Leadership separates formal authority from earned credibility and informal influence.

Opening these views does not alter the simulation. They report accumulated evidence and may remain uncertain when the company has not observed enough.

## Paper

The Paper screen contains Office Aquarium Weekly, the company's rule-based newspaper. A new issue is created every five simulated workdays from actual company events.

# 12. Company Metrics

## Hardware Product Health

The current health of active hardware-dependent products and projects. It combines reported execution health, quality, staffing coverage, blockers, risk, and relevant company conditions. It may show `N/A` when no real hardware product or project provides enough evidence.

Typical contributors include:

- Hardware Engineer
- Chip Architect
- Electrical Engineer
- Industrial Designer
- Manufacturing Engineer
- Software QA Engineer

## Software Product Health

The current health of active software- and firmware-dependent products and projects. It uses the same derived project evidence rather than a disconnected progress bar. It may show `N/A` when the company has no qualifying software work.

Typical contributors include:

- Software Engineer
- Firmware Engineer
- Software QA Engineer
- Technical Lead
- Software Architect

## Morale

Average employee morale.

Low morale increases the chance of complaints, conflict, poor performance, and resignation.

## Trust

Represents customer and public trust in the company and its product.

Trust is affected by:

- quality
- verification
- customer treatment
- launch decisions
- product defects
- broken promises

## Customers

The number of customers or customer commitments represented by the simulation.

Customer growth becomes more important during trials, pilots, and launch. Customers are not just a raw score: the company can attract different customer segments, and those segments may react differently to quality, price, support, reliability, trust, market timing, and product fit.

## Daily Revenue

Revenue generated by the product.

Revenue depends on:

- product phase
- customer count
- product readiness
- trust
- customer sentiment
- commercial project value
- support burden
- market conditions
- manufacturing fulfillment

Completed commercial projects can generate revenue when they have customer interest, product readiness, and enough delivery capability. A completed internal or research-heavy project may improve the company without immediately producing Daily Revenue.

## Manufacturing

Represents production readiness and fulfillment capability.

Manufacturing can be affected by:

- supply pressure
- yield
- capacity
- quality
- supplier decisions
- hardware readiness

## Investor Confidence

Represents how strongly investors and the outside market believe in the company's future. It is different from cash and Board Confidence.

Investor Confidence is shaped by:

- growth
- valuation
- revenue
- operating discipline
- risk
- long-term direction
- market conditions
- public project results
- fundraising and dilution

Valuation is a market estimate, not money in the bank. It can move because the outside world changes its view of the company. The valuation trend range controls use finance-style windows such as 1D, 1W, 1M, 3M, YTD, 1Y, and All. Changing the range changes the comparison window, so the same company can look flat over one day and meaningfully different over a month or a year.

## Team Cohesion

Shows how well teams are working together.

Cohesion is affected by:

- relationships
- collaboration
- stress
- conflict
- leadership decisions
- culture

## Customer Sentiment

Represents how customers currently feel about the company and product.

This is related to trust but focuses more directly on recent customer experience. It is weighted from customer satisfaction, willingness to expand, perceived reliability, support experience, delivery promises, and the needs of the customer segments the company is serving.

Customer count and Customer Sentiment can move separately. A company may gain customers while sentiment weakens if support, quality, or delivery falls behind. It may also have modest customer growth but strong sentiment if the product fits a smaller market well.

# 14. Employee AI

Employees are autonomous.

Each employee combines several decision systems.

## Needs

Employees monitor:

- energy
- stress
- morale
- focus

## Personality and Goals

Traits and personal goals affect behavior.

Goals include:

- mastery
- promotion
- social connection
- stability
- recognition

## Skills

Employees have role-related skills such as:

- architecture
- verification
- firmware
- software
- hardware
- leadership
- communication
- finance
- product

Skills affect work output and collaborator selection.

## Beliefs

Employees do not always have perfect information.

They form estimates about:

- product readiness
- risks
- team pressure
- blockers
- deadlines
- quality
- company conditions

Beliefs have confidence levels and information sources.

## Work and Intentions

Employees receive generated work items and form short intentions.

They may:

- inspect work
- coordinate with another employee
- execute a task
- verify results
- ask for help
- report a risk
- propose an opportunity

Work items now move through readable workflow stages instead of only showing a raw percentage:

- Investigation
- Requirements
- Implementation
- Testing or Review
- Ready for review
- Blocked
- Complete

When a work item is blocked, the department briefing and related reports name the blocker. When there is no blocker, employees may still request a peer review for skill gaps, deadline risk, or unclear requirements, but the report should not describe the situation as a false blocker.

## Relationships

Relationships are professional, not a friendship mini-game. The current social AI builds them in layers:

- familiarity from real encounters
- shared experience history from work, meetings, breaks, help, pressure, and milestones
- hidden relationship interpretation: trust, respect, comfort, and professional friction
- source-backed conflict and later repair attempts
- directional memory and contextual recall
- visible conversations grounded in real events
- slow culture, informal-group, and team-chemistry interpretation
- formal and informal leadership evidence

These hidden values affect how stressful or encouraging future interactions feel. They do not directly change project progress, quality, productivity, hiring, or company learning.

Employees choose collaborators mainly from project need, role usefulness, skill fit, availability, focus, stress, and social compatibility. A coworker can be trusted but still difficult to work with, or comfortable to talk to but not the most useful collaborator for a technical problem.

Conflict does not appear from nowhere. It can develop from a real disagreement, ignored request, interruption, credit dispute, deadline blame, failed collaboration, or dismissive exchange. A later apology, clarification, constructive follow-up, recognition, or successful joint effort may repair it, but employees do not accept every attempt.

Onboarding mentorship and conflict-repair conversations require the relevant employees to meet in the office. The visible exchange reports a real mentoring assignment or unresolved conflict; it does not create one merely because a speech bubble appeared.

Culture and informal groups emerge slowly from repeated evidence. Sharing a room does not create a friendship, clique, conflict, or leadership reputation by itself. A manager has formal authority, but credibility must still be earned; a non-manager can become an informal leader through consistent mentoring, communication, and conflict handling.

## Memories

Employees remember important events, including:

- CEO support
- cost cuts
- overtime
- collaboration
- leadership pressure
- successful or harmful decisions
- meaningful conflict and repair
- mentoring, recognition, and repeated shared outcomes

Social memory is selective. Routine memories fade or compress over time, while important unresolved experiences can remain influential. Two employees may remember the same event differently because their personalities and prior relationships differ.

## Learning

Employees adapt from completed outcomes.

An employee may gradually become more likely to:

- ask for help
- test earlier
- collaborate
- take initiative
- report risks
- suppress reports
- recover before burnout
- behave cautiously
- accept risk

# 15. Institutional Learning

The company does not only remember events. It can learn from them.

Institutional Learning turns reviewed outcomes into lessons that may influence future behavior. Evidence can come from CEO decisions, launches, pilot choices, coaching, firing, burnout resignations, quality mistakes, successful collaboration, and long-term company patterns.

Lessons can affect:

- departments
- veteran employees
- new hires through onboarding
- future employee action choices
- company culture over time

Employees do not all accept lessons equally. Personality, department, CEO trust, and direct experience affect how strongly a person internalizes a lesson. Someone who lived through a crisis may learn from it more deeply than a new hire who only hears about it later.

The Lessons Learned panel shows the strongest institutional lessons currently shaping the company.

Lessons are evidence-based. The company does not learn just because a policy was approved, a countdown changed, a memo appeared, or a transition finished. Medium- and long-term reviews determine whether evidence is provisional, validated, contradicted, obsolete, or too weak to matter. Repeated reviews of the same episode do not count as independent proof.

The same rule applies to specialized project and workforce learning. Opening recruiting, starting coaching, or approving a project action does not prove success. Completed onboarding, completed projects, successful recovery, and reviewed delayed consequences can become learning evidence.

# 16. Internal Communication and Escalation

Employees generate their own internal communications.

Possible report types include:

- help requests
- status reports
- risk reports
- opportunity proposals
- rumors
- suppressed reports

Not every issue reaches the CEO.

The organization routes information through several levels:

1. handled personally
2. handled within a team
3. recorded as an executive information memo
4. escalated as a CEO decision

Strategic, expensive, company-wide, or high-risk issues are most likely to reach your inbox.

This means the CEO Inbox is partly created by the employees' observations, beliefs, confidence, fear, trust, and reporting behavior.

# 17. Making CEO Decisions

A decision usually trades one benefit for another.

Common tensions include:

- speed versus quality
- cash versus morale
- growth versus stability
- customer promises versus technical readiness
- investor pressure versus long-term research
- accountability versus recovery
- launch timing versus product risk

Before deciding, review:

- memo evidence
- confidence level
- sender recommendation
- other department viewpoints
- no-action forecast
- uncertainty
- employee stress
- cash
- product phase
- quality
- trust
- manufacturing
- recent story threads

There is rarely a perfect choice.

CEO choices are strategic estimates, not guaranteed outcomes. The same choice can perform differently depending on the company's cash, quality, integration, stress, morale, manufacturing readiness, customer sentiment, market demand, and competitor pressure.

Most employee-originated concerns reach you through a manager, executive, operating council, or the board. The memo may name the employee who first raised the issue, but routine workload concerns usually do not bypass management. Direct employee-to-CEO messages are reserved for rare severe or protected situations.

Some decisions create delayed consequences. A choice that looks good today may create a drawback several days later, and a risky choice may sometimes work if the company has the right conditions. Delayed outcomes can appear later in Company Story, the weekly newspaper, Company History, Institutional Learning, and archived CEO communications.

Archived CEO communications can also show a decision thread. A decision thread records how the organization reacted, when operational follow-through began, what larger consequences appeared, and whether the decision became part of the company's long-term memory. Not every update creates a new Inbox interruption; many follow-ups are recorded as "No action required" so the company can keep running.

Decisions also leave a leadership footprint. Over time, the game tracks whether your leadership tends toward quality, speed, innovation, employee wellbeing, financial discipline, customer focus, transparency, risk tolerance, accountability, and long-term thinking. Employees react to that accumulated pattern, not only to the latest memo.

Your decisions create directives that may temporarily shift company behavior toward:

- speed
- quality
- people
- cost cutting
- revenue

Employees react according to their own personality, goals, memories, and opinion of you.

Every quarter, the board reviews the organization. These reviews look at people, execution, retention risk, leadership consistency, and company momentum. Employees also receive quarterly review data that can affect promotion expectations, recognition, retention, and future behavior.

The board may also issue hiring, restructuring, layoff, or CEO performance-improvement recommendations. You still do not directly pick employees to hire or lay off. The organization makes a recommendation and you choose the strategic response.

# 18. Common CEO Situations

## Engineering Workload Warning

You may:

- delay
- approve headcount or outside support
- continue pushing

Watch stress, morale, quality, and cash.

## Prototype Breakthrough

You may:

- begin testing
- accelerate
- continue research

This affects schedule, quality, trust, and board confidence.

## Launch Authorization

You may:

- launch fully
- run a pilot
- keep polishing

A full launch offers higher growth but more risk.

## Cash Runway Concern

You may:

- cut costs
- raise funding
- focus on revenue

Cost cutting can create resentment and long-term cultural damage.

## Performance Review

You may:

- support a recovery plan
- approve reassignment or coverage
- approve termination and backfill if the issue has escalated that far

Performance reviews are based on sustained output problems, absence, mistakes, role pressure, and recovery history.

## Hiring Vacancy

You may:

- approve a role for HR to fill
- support an internal promotion path
- approve contractors or temporary coverage

HR, managers, and departments handle the actual candidate search and onboarding unless an exception becomes CEO-level. Each option changes capability, workload, morale, and cash differently.

## Market or Supplier Pressure

You may:

- pursue demand
- protect reliability
- reposition
- accept supply risk
- change manufacturing plans

# 22. Internal Reports

Internal Reports expose selected employee-generated information.

This panel is not a full task board. It is a recent report feed: what employees noticed, asked for, or escalated. If a report is connected to a work item, the card shows the current live work status, owner, deadline, and blocker information so older reports do not look like frozen task data.

Reports can help explain:

- who noticed a problem
- what evidence they had
- confidence in the report
- severity and urgency
- whether the issue was escalated
- whether it was handled locally

Suppressed or delayed reports can still affect CEO memos, Company Story, the Weekly Newspaper, sender credibility, manager credibility, and Institutional Learning when relevant. They are no longer shown as a permanent raw count or separate dashboard.

Use internal reports to understand how information moves through the company.

# 23. Company Story and Story Threads

The Company Story log records important events.

Story Threads connect related events into longer chains.

Example:

Supplier delay  
-> hardware work blocked  
-> firmware integration slows  
-> QA reports risk  
-> CEO chooses a pilot  
-> newspaper reports the outcome

Story threads help you see cause and effect across several simulated days.

# 24. Company History

Company History is the long-term biography of your organization.

The newspaper reports recent weeks. Story Threads show active chains. Company History keeps only the major milestones that define the company over time.

It may include:

- phase changes
- important CEO decisions
- major resignations or firings
- hiring milestones
- successful launches
- revenue turning positive
- customer milestones
- manufacturing disruptions
- investor pressure
- major story threads

The goal is not to record everything. The goal is to let a 300-day or 500-day company look back on its own legacy, almost like reading a short company biography.

- lead headline

# 26. Crisis and Failure

The company can enter crisis because of:

- cash failure
- extremely low board confidence
- extreme employee stress
- very low trust
- too few active employees

During a crisis, you receive a limited recovery period.

To recover, improve the dangerous conditions before the deadline.

If recovery fails, the board removes you as CEO and the game ends.

Typical recovery priorities:

1. restore cash
2. reduce stress
3. rebuild trust
4. maintain enough active employees
5. raise board confidence

# 29. Strategy Tips

## Early Game

- protect cash
- avoid excessive speed directives
- build chip and software together
- watch integration before launch
- prevent sustained employee stress

## Mid Game

- resolve blockers
- use useful collaborators
- maintain quality
- watch customer sentiment
- avoid repeated cost cutting

## Launch

- do not judge readiness from one metric
- check integration, quality, trust, manufacturing, and team pressure
- use a pilot when uncertainty is high

## Long-Term

- keep employees healthy enough to retain experience
- support high-value employees without ignoring poor performance
- balance shareholder needs with sustainable operations
- watch trends, not just current values
- read story threads to understand hidden causes

# 30. What Success Looks Like

Winning is not just making money.

A company can have record revenue and still be unhealthy. It can grow quickly while employees burn out, quality falls, customers lose trust, and culture becomes brittle. That kind of company may look successful for a while, but it is fragile.

Likewise, a company can make mistakes and still become strong if it learns.

A lasting company can:

- recover from bad decisions
- retain talented people
- build customer trust
- improve product quality
- survive cash pressure
- communicate honestly
- turn internal problems into better strategy
- launch when the organization is ready
- create stories that feel earned

Your goal is not simply to maximize profit. Your goal is to build a healthy company that survives, adapts, and creates its own stories.

The best runs are not always the cleanest runs. Sometimes the most memorable company is the one that almost failed, learned from the damage, kept its people, rebuilt trust, and launched anyway.

# 31. Office Aquarium Design Philosophy

Office Aquarium is built around one principle:

A company is a collection of people, not numbers.

The simulation exists to model how information, decisions, relationships, and leadership interact over time. Employees are not scripted actors waiting for commands. They observe, communicate, learn, adapt, disagree, recover, and sometimes fail.

Your role is not to control them.

Your role is to create the conditions in which they, and the company, can succeed.

That is why the game emphasizes imperfect information, indirect leadership, internal communication, culture, memory, and consequences. The most important question is not always "How do I make the number go up?" Sometimes it is "What kind of organization am I creating by making this decision?"

# 32. Troubleshooting

## The simulation appears stopped

Check:

- whether Pause is active
- whether a CEO decision is waiting
- whether the game-over screen is open
- whether the browser tab has suspended background activity

## No CEO messages are appearing

The company may be operating normally.

Messages are generated from:

- strategic thresholds
- employee reports
- escalated issues
- market opportunities
- performance concerns
- hiring needs

## The same type of message appears repeatedly

Review the underlying condition.

Examples:

- cash remains low
- performance risk remains unresolved
- stress is still high
- a vacancy remains open

The event scheduler uses cooldowns and novelty weighting, but unresolved conditions may return.

## My save is missing

Possible causes:

- browser storage was cleared
- the file is being opened in a different browser
- private browsing was used
- the browser blocks local storage for local files

## Mobile layout feels crowded

Scroll the top tab bar horizontally and open one section at a time.

## I cannot hear sound

Check the sound dropdown in the Office controls. `Muted` disables all sound, `Alerts Only` keeps message notifications without music, `Music Only` plays the background loop without message alerts, and `Music + Alerts` enables both.

Some browsers block sound until you interact with the page. If the audio files are missing or the browser does not allow local audio, the game will keep running silently.
